The Journey

Two cities. Three rounds. One unforgettable introduction.

There is a right way to see China for the first time, and it runs from north to south — from the capital of emperors to the city of the future. This journey holds both, stitched together by a single high-speed train ride, and threads three of the country's finest golf courses through the icons in between.

Beijing opens the trip the way it has opened China for six centuries: the Forbidden City before the crowds, the hutong lanes where the city still lives, and a round at Reignwood Pine Valley — Jack Nicklaus fairways laid beneath the Great Wall itself, at a club whose membership is otherwise closed to the world.

Then the country changes around you. Four hours south by rail, Shanghai delivers the counterpoint: tournament golf at Sheshan International, where a world-tour event has crowned champions for two decades, and a wild links among the dunes of the Yangtze estuary that feels closer to Ireland than to anything else in Asia. Between rounds — the Bund, the old concessions, and some of the best tables in the world.

Day by Day

The shape of the journey.

Eight nights, two cities, three rounds — a rhythm of golf, icons and unhurried time that has been refined across many first visits. Yours will be redrawn around your dates and your party.

BeijingNights 1–4
Day 1 Arrive Beijing

Landing in the capital of emperors.

Met on arrival with fast-track clearance and a private transfer into the old city, where your hotel waits among the hutong lanes a few streets from the Forbidden City. The evening is deliberately gentle: a first walk through the grey-brick alleys as the lanterns come on, and a welcome dinner of crisp-skinned Peking duck at a courtyard table.

Private arrival & fast-trackWelcome dinner
Day 2 Imperial Beijing

First through the Meridian Gate.

Into the Forbidden City as its gates open, with a historian who reads the architecture like a text — the courtyards still empty, the light still low. The afternoon belongs to the living city: morning-market lanes, a chef's-table lunch in a private courtyard house, and time to settle into Beijing's rhythm before the golf begins.

Day 3 Round One

Golf beneath the Great Wall.

The round the journey is built around. At Reignwood Pine Valley, Nicklaus fairways climb through the Jundu Mountains with the Wall tracing the ridgeline above the back nine — a closely-held private club where we arrange guest play on your behalf, with caddies, forecaddies and a quiet clubhouse lunch beneath the château's chandeliers.

Day 4 The Wall

A wild stretch of Wall to yourselves.

North into the mountains for the day the non-golfers will talk about longest: a private walk along an unrestored stretch of the Great Wall, no crowds and no barriers, finishing with a table set on the ramparts as the light goes gold. Down the mountain by dark, back to the courtyard for a nightcap.

Optional add-on · A fourth round

Morning golf at Topwin, then the Wall.

Golfers can open the day at Topwin Golf & Country Club on Yanqi Lake — the Woosnam course played by President Obama, twenty minutes from the Wall at Mutianyu — and still make the ramparts for dinner.

Day 5 Beijing → Shanghai

South, at 350 kilometres an hour.

A last unhurried morning — the Temple of Heaven at opening time, or nothing at all — then the business-class cabin of a high-speed train south. Four and a half hours later the skyline of Pudong rises over the Huangpu, and your hotel on the Bund is pouring the first drink of the second act. Dinner above the lights of two skylines.

Day 6 Round Two

Tournament golf at Sheshan.

China's most famous course, and for two decades host of its flagship world-tour event — the closing stretch around the old quarry has decided more champions than any other holes in Asia. A strictly private members' club; we arrange your game, your caddies and a long lunch on the terrace where the trophies are handed out. Evening among the plane trees of the old French Concession.

Day 7 Round Three

A wild links at the mouth of the Yangtze.

An hour from the Bund, on Chongming Island, the trip's final round is its biggest surprise: Yangtze Dunes, a true links through native dunes and wetland, firm turf and river wind, ranked among China's top three modern courses. The world's busiest shipping lane glides past beyond the dunes. Back in the city by evening for a farewell feast.

The city, arranged around you.

A day deliberately left open, then built to order: contemporary art in the West Bund museums, a tailor's fitting on the Bund, architecture from art-deco to the 632-metre Shanghai Tower, or simply the city's best kitchens back to back. The Golf

Three rounds, three different Chinas.

Mountain golf beneath the Wall, tournament turf outside Shanghai and a wild river links — the strongest possible first hand of golf in China.

Round 1 · Signature
Jundu Mountains · Beijing

Reignwood Pine Valley

Set beneath the Great Wall near Badaling, Reignwood Pine Valley is one of China's most private and prestigious golf clubs — a Nicklaus mountain-parkland layout where elevated tees, sculpted bunkering and fast, guarded greens unfold below the Jundu Mountains, the Wall tracing the ridgeline above. Membership is closely held; we arrange guest play on your behalf.

Sheshan International

China's most famous course, and for two decades the host of its flagship world-tour event — Tiger Woods, Rory McIlroy and Phil Mickelson have all chased titles over the closing holes around the old quarry. A Nelson & Haworth parkland of mature camphors and a château clubhouse, strictly private; your game is arranged on your behalf, with lunch on the trophy terrace.

Round 3 · Wild links
Chongming Island · Shanghai

Yangtze Dunes

An hour from the Bund, on an island at the mouth of the Yangtze, lies the most surprising round in China — a true links running through native dunes and wetlands, shaped by the celebrated Australian firm OCM. Firm turf, river wind, blind shoulders and greens tucked into the sand: golf that feels closer to the Irish coast than to anything else in Asia.

Where You Stay

Two addresses, two Chinas.

Four nights inside Beijing's oldest lanes, four above Shanghai's grandest waterfront — each hotel part of the journey itself.

Beijing · Hutong Courtyards

Sleep where the city is oldest

Our Beijing favourites sit in the hutongs, a few lanes from the Forbidden City — the likes of the Waldorf Astoria's courtyard houses, where grey brick and bronze doors give way to deep baths and very good martinis. Step out of the gate and the oldest streets in the capital are yours before breakfast.

For travelers who prefer glass and skyline to grey brick, the city's modern landmarks along the central axis make an equally good base.

Shanghai · On the Bund

A front-row seat on the Huangpu

In Shanghai the address is the experience: a grand house on the Bund itself, where restored colonial banking halls meet river-view suites and the two skylines face off outside your window. The old concessions, the best tables in the city and your tailor are all within a few minutes' walk.

Prefer the future to the past? The towers of Pudong hold some of the highest hotel rooms on earth — we'll match the side of the river to your taste.

Specific hotels, room categories and the level of comfort you choose are set out in your private proposal — every address booked entirely on your behalf.
